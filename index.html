Overview

Abstract

Computer vision for sports still lacks datasets and models that target pole sports, despite challenges such as self-occlusion, body inversions, and sustained contact with the pole. Pole-Arina addresses this gap with a curated, privacy-preserving dataset for markerless analysis of static pole tricks.

The benchmark contains 836 videos from 58 participants and exposes two appearance-suppressing modalities derived from the recordings: pose skeleton sequences and dense optical flow. Each clip is annotated with both per-video trick labels and per-frame temporal labels, enabling clip-level and frame-wise recognition under strong privacy constraints.

On top of the dataset, the paper benchmarks lightweight temporal baselines across both modalities and both supervision granularities, then extends the pipeline toward interpretable, coaching-oriented feedback through a geometric scoring module and a user study.

Method

From Video to Recognition and Feedback

The benchmark starts from recorded training clips but releases only derived, privacy-preserving features. Body skeletons capture structured pose and confidence over time, while dense optical flow emphasizes motion without preserving identity.

Lightweight GRU-based temporal models provide a consistent baseline across modalities and label granularities. The same pipeline also supports a downstream coaching prototype, where predicted trick labels and end-pose geometry become explicit feedback rules.

Key takeaways

Skeletons provide a strong inductive bias for pose-centric recognition and clearly outperform optical flow at the clip level. Frame-wise recognition remains harder, especially under label imbalance, but the benchmark still supports meaningful temporal understanding from privacy-preserving motion alone.

The paper highlights especially strong skeleton-based performance for several trick classes, while optical flow remains useful for dominant support states such as floor and on_pole.

Application

Coaching-Oriented Feedback Prototype

Pole-Arina is not only a recognition benchmark. The paper also demonstrates a practical downstream workflow where the predicted trick and end-pose geometry feed into a coaching-style interface for structured review.

In the reported between-subjects study, the experimental condition achieved a higher SUS score than simple smartphone self-review (95.44 +/- 4.07 vs. 86.41 +/- 11.14, p = .020).

Interpretability 5-7 rules per trick

Angles, alignments, and joint proximities stay human-readable.
